Early June Conditions

The San Juan Mountains are still shedding snowpack in early June. Trails above 9,500 ft may have significant snow, icy patches, and cold stream crossings. Always call the Pagosa Ranger District before heading to high-elevation routes.

01
San Juan Riverwalk

A paved path along the San Juan River winding through downtown Pagosa Springs. Completely flat, stroller-friendly, and dotted with benches and fishing access points. The best option for young kids or anyone who wants a morning walk with coffee. Connects Town Park, Yamaguchi Park, and Mary Fisher Park.

Easy ~2 mi Flat Paved path 🎣 Fish spots 01–03
Directions to Town Park Fishing: San Juan Town Park
02
Reservoir Hill Trail System

A network of trails right behind town, accessible from Hot Springs Blvd or behind the San Juan Motel. Quick elevation gain earns panoramic views of Pagosa Peak and the San Juan Valley. Multiple loop options from 1 to 4 miles. Popular with trail runners in the morning. Restored habitat draws migratory birds in June.

Easy–Moderate 1–4 mi loops ~400 ft gain Dirt trail
Directions to Reservoir Hill AllTrails
03
Coyote Hill Loop

An accessible loop off Piedra Road with sweeping views of the San Juan Mountains and Pagosa Peak. Easily done clockwise or counterclockwise. Low elevation keeps it free of snow in early June. Can link to Leche Creek Trail or V Rock Trail for a longer outing. Watch for deer and wild turkey at dawn.

Easy 2.5 mi loop ~300 ft gain Loop · Views

04
Fourmile Falls

One of the most rewarding moderate hikes near Pagosa. A well-marked trail through aspen and conifer leads to a spectacular double waterfall in a rocky amphitheater. Views of Eagle Mountain (12,007 ft) and Pagosa Peak (12,640 ft) along the way. The falls are at peak flow in early June — dramatic. Take CR 400 north from Hwy 160 about 8 miles, then Fourmile Road another 5 miles to the trailhead.

Moderate 6 mi RT ~600 ft gain Waterfall · Views
Directions to trailhead AllTrails
05
Piedra River Trail — Family Section

The upper trailhead of the Piedra River Trail offers an incredible introductory hike into a sandstone box canyon. Half a mile down trail the river enters sheer canyon walls — great picnic spot with shade. Climber's pitons visible on the cliffs. Another half mile reaches the river bend; 1.5 miles reaches the Williams Creek bridge — a perfect 3–4 mile RT family hike. The fishing group can wade the Piedra while hikers explore the trail simultaneously.

Moderate 3–4 mi RT ~500 ft gain Canyon · River 🎣 Fish spots 05, 10
Trailhead directions Fishing: Piedra River Lower Canyon
06
Cimarrona Creek Trail

A beautiful 6.5-mile one-way trail winding through conifers and aspen, accessed off Williams Lake Road (4.1 miles past the Cimarrona Campground entrance). Trailhead sits at 8,400 ft. Runs all the way to Squaw Pass on the Continental Divide Trail for those with a shuttle. Lower sections are clear in early June; upper sections may have snow. The creek itself is beautiful — worth going just 2–3 miles in.

Moderate 6.5 mi OW ~800 ft gain Creek · Forest

07
Turkey Creek Trail

Begins at Jackson Mountain and runs to Piedra Pass on the Continental Divide Trail. ATVs allowed the first 3 miles, then foot and horse only — that's where the trail becomes magic. Cutthroat trout in Turkey Creek draw anglers and hikers alike. "Worth the walk to have the beauty of nature around you," says one local. Don't go too deep without a map; fish the river to the right when you cross the small creek past the cattle guard.

Strenuous 10+ mi ~1,600 ft gain Creek · CDT access 🎣 Fish spot 11
Trailhead directions Fishing: Turkey Creek
08
Full Piedra River Trail

One of the outstanding wilderness trails in the southern Rockies. An 11-mile one-way route clinging to the Piedra as it carves deep box canyons — accessible only on foot or by whitewater craft. Along the way: Williams Creek confluence, Weminuche Creek, Sand Creek. Wild cutthroat and brown trout in nearly every pool. Shuttle required for full traverse. Upper section easy for the first 3.5 miles before going moderate.

Strenuous 11 mi OW ~1,400 ft gain Canyon · River · Remote 🎣 Fish spots 05, 10
Upper trailhead directions AllTrails
09
West Fork / Rainbow Trail

11.75 miles one-way from Hwy 160 NE of Pagosa Springs. Take Forest Service Road 648, past West Fork Campground, crossing the West Fork of the San Juan River and Beaver Creek — both crossings can be serious in early June runoff, proceed with caution. Hot springs are accessible about 5 miles up but off the beaten path — check with the Forest Service for exact location. Spectacular alpine scenery.

Strenuous 11.75 mi OW ~2,000 ft gain Hot springs · Alpine
Trailhead directions
10
Williams Creek Trail (Lower)

Access from the end of Williams Lake Road. The full trail ascends 3,400 feet over 14 miles to the Continental Divide — but going 3–5 miles in and out is deeply satisfying on its own. The Indian Creek Trail junction at mile 2.5 offers a side trip. Williams Lake Trail branches at mile 7.5. The creek corridor follows some of the most beautiful stream fishing water in the area — the lower 4 miles overlap with the Williams Creek fishing access.

Strenuous 9.6 mi to CDT ~2,200 ft gain Creek · Wilderness 🎣 Fish spot 06
Trailhead directions Fishing: Williams Creek AllTrails
11
Williams Creek → Continental Divide

The full commitment: 14 miles and 3,400 vertical feet from the Williams Lake Road trailhead to the Continental Divide at 11,800 ft. Three steep sections, two stream crossings that may be impassable in early June. The views from the divide stretch into the Weminuche Wilderness — one of the largest wilderness areas in the country. Backpack or very long day. Contact the Pagosa Ranger District before attempting; check snowpack reports.

Expert 14 mi OW 3,400 ft gain Wilderness · CDT 🎣 Fish spot 06
Trailhead directions Fishing: Williams Creek
12
Blanco Basin / Castle Creek

Head south on Hwy 84 about 8 miles to Blanco Basin Road, east to Castle Creek, right across the Blanco River, then 2 miles to a signed parking area. The trail corridor follows the crystalline Blanco River, which reviewers rave about for its clarity. A beautiful, lightly pressured area perfect for a half-day hike and swim combination in a forested setting east of Pagosa.

Moderate Variable ~600 ft gain River · Forest 🎣 Fish spot 08

What to Bring

Gear for Every Trail

Early June in the San Juans means variable conditions — warm afternoons in town, cold afternoons above 9,000 ft, afternoon thunderstorms almost daily. Pack accordingly regardless of the forecast.

The Ten Essentials
Always-carry list
  • Navigation — map + downloaded AllTrails route (no signal in canyons)
  • Water — 2L minimum per person, filter or tablets as backup
  • Food — more than you think; altitude hits hard
  • Sun protection — SPF 50 and sunglasses, high-altitude UV is intense
  • Insulation — packable down layer even on warm days
  • Headlamp — for late returns or early starts
  • First aid kit — blister care is the #1 trail issue
  • Fire starter — waterproof matches + lighter
  • Repair tools + knife
  • Emergency shelter — emergency bivy under 4 oz
Early June Specific
Seasonal additions
  • Rain shell — afternoon thunderstorms arrive fast above treeline
  • Microspikes or trekking poles — above 9,500 ft expect ice
  • Gaiters — snowmelt makes the trail edges muddy
  • Waterproof hiking boots — stream crossings are cold and fast
  • Warm hat and gloves — summit temperatures 20°F colder than town
  • Dry bag or pack liner — afternoon showers are the norm
  • Bug repellent — mosquitoes emerge early June near water
Lightning Protocol: if you hear thunder, descend immediately. Above treeline you have 0 shelter. Get below trees before storms develop — usually by 2pm.
Kids & Families
Making it work for everyone
  • Extra snacks — kids burn calories fast at altitude
  • Moleskin and blister kit — kids rarely speak up until it's bad
  • Sun hoodies — easier than reapplying sunscreen all day
  • Trekking poles — kids 5+ benefit on descent
  • Small daypack for each kid — kids hike better with some responsibility
  • Whistle for each child
Best family trails: Riverwalk (01), Reservoir Hill (02), Fourmile Falls (04), and the short Piedra River family section (05) — all under 4 miles with good turnaround points.
Wildlife Awareness
Know before you go
  • Bear spray — recommended on any trail in the Weminuche
  • Make noise — bears are active in June after hibernation
  • Elk calving season — give elk space; cows are aggressive with calves
  • Rattlesnakes — watch for them on warm south-facing rocks at lower elevations
  • Mountain lions — extremely rare but present; hike in groups
  • Deer flies — June through July near water, annoying but harmless
No trash — pack everything out. Weminuche Wilderness Leave No Trace standards apply to all connected trails.

Local Knowledge

Resources & Trail Orgs

Call the ranger district before any high-elevation hike in early June. Trail conditions change daily with snowmelt and afternoon storms. These are the people and tools to check first.

Pagosa Ranger District

San Juan National Forest's Pagosa office. Current trail conditions, fire restrictions, wilderness permits, and road closures. Essential call before any strenuous or high-elevation hike in early June.

(970) 264-2268 fs.usda.gov/sanjuan
Pagosa Trails

The regional volunteer trail organization maintaining and mapping trails around Pagosa Springs. Detailed trail profiles, current conditions blog, and volunteer opportunities. Best local source for real-time trail updates.

pagosatrails.org
