In Town
Within walking distance · no drive required
01
San Juan River — Town Park

The most accessible stretch in Pagosa. The San Juan runs right through Town Park with easy gravel bar access and good riffles. Expect smaller fish but great practice water — and you're steps from coffee. Stocked and wild fish mix here.

Rainbow · Brown Riffle · Pool In Town Easy 🥾 Riverwalk hike
Directions Hike: San Juan Riverwalk
02
San Juan River — Mary Fisher Park

Across the river from Town Park with direct bank access and a bit less foot traffic. Connects to the San Juan River Walk. Shallow riffles good for sight fishing on calm mornings. Free parking.

Rainbow · Brown Riffle · Bank In Town Easy
Directions
03
San Juan River — Yamaguchi Park

Quiet south-end park with direct river access and a walking path. Less crowded than Town Park, with a slightly deeper run along the bank. Good early morning spot before the town wakes up.

Rainbow · Brown Deep Run · Bank In Town Easy
Directions
15–45 min
Short drives with excellent wild trout fishing
04
East Fork San Juan River

One of the best kept secrets near Pagosa. Beautiful pocket water with wild rainbows and cutthroat in a forested canyon setting. Multiple access points off FR 667 north of town. Some sections flow through private ranch — stay on public land.

Rainbow · Cutthroat Pocket Water · Meadow ~20 min N Moderate
Directions
05
Piedra River — Lower Canyon

Wild rainbow and brown trout in a dramatic red-rock canyon. Access via Piedra Road (CR 600) west of town. Multiple pullouts with wade access to beautiful runs and pools. One of the most scenic stretches of water in the area.

Rainbow · Brown Canyon · Pool & Run ~25 min W Moderate 🥾 Piedra Trail nearby
Directions Hike: Piedra River Trail
06
Williams Creek

Feeds Williams Creek Reservoir through the national forest. A small-stream fishery with good populations of brook, rainbow, cutthroat, and the rare tiger trout. Multiple access points along the forest road. Tight casting — 4-weight territory.

Brook · Cutthroat · Tiger Small Stream ~30 min N Easy–Moderate 🥾 Williams Creek Trail
Directions Hike: Williams Creek Trail
07
Echo Canyon Reservoir SWA

State-stocked trout lake right off Hwy 84 with easy bank access and sweeping mountain views. Great for a half day. Reviewers call the fishing "fantastic." Technically still water but wading the shallows at the inlet is productive.

Rainbow · Brook Still Water · Bank ~20 min SE Easy
Directions
08
Blanco River

A beautiful, lightly pressured freestone stream east of Pagosa along FR 656. Wild rainbow and brown trout in a forested setting. The Blanco River Group Campground sits right on the water — camping and fishing in one. Reviewers rave about the river's clarity.

Rainbow · Brown Freestone · Riffle ~35 min E Easy 🥾 Blanco Basin hike
Directions Hike: Blanco Basin / Castle Creek
09
Navajo River

A smaller, remote stream southwest of Pagosa near the New Mexico border. Good wild cutthroat and rainbow populations in a quiet valley. Light pressure makes this a gem for those willing to explore. Check access before heading out.

Cutthroat · Rainbow Small Stream ~40 min SW Moderate
Directions
45 min + · Hike-In
Worth the drive — remote, uncrowded, exceptional
10
Piedra River — Upper Trail

The Piedra River Trail follows the upper canyon for 10–15 miles with multiple river access points. Wild cutthroat and brown trout in crystal-clear pools. Reviewers have spotted river otters. The deeper you hike, the less pressure you'll find. Bring felt boots and a net.

Cutthroat · Brown Canyon · Hike-In ~45 min NW + hike Strenuous 🥾 Piedra River Trail
Trailhead Directions Hike: Full Piedra River Trail
11
Turkey Creek

A rewarding hike-in fishery north of Pagosa. Cutthroat and good trout fishing in a quiet creek setting. "Worth the walk to have the beauty of nature around you," says one local. Don't go too deep on the trail — fish the river to the right when you cross the small creek past the cattle guard.

Cutthroat · Trout Creek · Hike-In ~40 min N + hike Moderate–Strenuous 🥾 Turkey Creek Trail
Directions Hike: Turkey Creek Trail
12
Fish Creek

A small, remote creek east of town accessible via forest roads. Light pressure and native cutthroat in a pristine setting. "The drive to the trailhead is reason enough to visit," per one reviewer. Watch for deer flies in summer.

Cutthroat Small Creek · Remote ~50 min E Moderate
Directions
13
San Juan River — Texas Hole

The crown jewel — a world-class tailwater below Navajo Dam, ~1.5 hrs south into New Mexico. Legendary 18"+ rainbows and browns stacked in the runs. Go early before it gets crowded. Some of the best catch rates of any river in the Southwest. Full-day float or wade.

Rainbow · Brown 18"+ Tailwater · World Class ~1.5 hrs S (NM) Easy Wade
Directions

Guide Fly Patterns · SW Colorado

Flies to Tie

These are the patterns local guides reach for first — quick ties with minimal materials that consistently put fish in the net on the San Juan, Piedra, East Fork, and Blanco. Each includes a link to a tying tutorial.

01 San Juan Worm Worm
Red · Pink · Tan  ·  #10–14  ·  3 materials

The #1 guide fly on the San Juan tailwater — period. An annelid worm imitation that fish can't resist year-round. Dead simple: hook, thread, ultra chenille. Guides tie these by the dozen the night before a float. Works on every river in this area.

Materials: Hook · Red ultra chenille · Thread
YouTube Tutorial Orvis Pattern
02 Zebra Midge Nymph
Black / Silver · Red / Silver  ·  #18–22  ·  3 materials

The essential tailwater midge for the San Juan River. Midges make up a huge percentage of the trout's diet below Navajo Dam year-round. The Zebra Midge is a guide staple: bead, wire rib, thread body. Tie it in black/silver and red/silver — carry both.

Materials: Silver bead · Black thread · Fine silver wire
YouTube Tutorial MidCurrent
03 RS2 Nymph / Emerger
Gray · Olive · Black  ·  #18–22  ·  4 materials

Created specifically for the San Juan River by Rim Chung in the 1970s. This midge/BWO emerger pattern is in every Southwest guide's box. Fished in the film or just below, it imitates a midge pupa or small mayfly emerger perfectly. Minimal materials, maximum fish.

Materials: Hook · Gray dubbing · Beaver or superfine · Micro fibers (tail) · Antron wing
YouTube Tutorial Fly Fish Food
04 Copper John Nymph
Copper · Red · Green  ·  #12–18  ·  5 materials

John Barr's classic attractor nymph is one of the best-selling flies in history for good reason — it sinks fast, produces flash, and triggers strikes even when nothing is hatching. Deadly on the Piedra, East Fork, and Blanco. The wire body is faster to tie than it looks.

Materials: Bead · Lead wire · Copper wire · Peacock herl · Pheasant tail · Epoxy back
YouTube Tutorial Orvis Pattern
05 Pheasant Tail Nymph Nymph
Natural · #14–18  ·  3 materials

Frank Sawyer's 1950s classic remains one of the most effective nymphs ever tied. Imitates PMDs, baetis, and small mayfly nymphs — all of which hatch in June on every stream in this area. Bead-head version sinks faster and gets down in the Piedra's faster runs.

Materials: Copper bead · Pheasant tail fibers · Copper wire
YouTube Tutorial Tightline Video
06 Hare's Ear Nymph Nymph
Natural Tan · #12–16  ·  4 materials

The "close enough" fly that imitates just about everything — caddis pupae, mayfly nymphs, small stoneflies. Guides trust it because the scruffy hare's ear dubbing breathes and moves in current. Deadly on the Blanco and Williams Creek where fish are opportunistic feeders.

Materials: Gold bead · Hare's ear dubbing · Gold wire rib · Pheasant tail wing case
YouTube Tutorial Fly Fish Food
07 Elk Hair Caddis Dry
Tan · Olive · #14–16  ·  4 materials

Al Troth's classic is the go-to dry for June caddis hatches across SW Colorado. Elk hair floats high and rides the riffles perfectly. The palmered hackle makes it visible and buoyant. Guides tie this pattern in bulk — it's that reliable on the Piedra and East Fork.

Materials: Elk hair wing · Brown hackle · Tan dubbing · Thread
YouTube Tutorial Orvis Pattern
08 Parachute Adams Dry
Gray · #14–18  ·  5 materials

The most versatile dry fly ever tied. The white parachute post makes it visible at distance and in riffles, while the gray body imitates everything from PMDs to BWOs to spent caddis. Any guide who only brings one dry fly brings this one. Works on every river in this area.

Materials: Grizzly hackle · Adams gray dubbing · White calf tail post · Moose mane tail · Thread
YouTube Tutorial Orvis Pattern
09 Stimulator Dry
Yellow / Orange  ·  #10–14  ·  5 materials

Randall Kaufmann's attractor pattern imitates a Golden Stonefly adult — one of the most important hatches on the Piedra and East Fork in June. It also works as a hopper or general attractor when nothing obvious is hatching. The high-riding elk hair wing makes it easy to see in fast water.

Materials: Elk hair wing · Yellow/orange dubbing · Grizzly hackle (palmered) · Elk hair tail
YouTube Tutorial Fly Fish Food
10 Woolly Bugger Streamer
Olive · Black · Brown  ·  #6–10  ·  3 materials

The most versatile wet fly ever tied. Imitates leeches, crayfish, sculpin, or a wounded baitfish depending on how you fish it. Strip it slow through deep San Juan pools at dawn for big browns, or dead-drift it through Piedra canyon runs. Olive with a bead head is the guide standard.

Materials: Marabou tail · Olive chenille body · Palmered saddle hackle · Gold bead (optional)
YouTube Tutorial Orvis Pattern

The regional TU chapter based in Durango (~60 miles away). Active chapter with stream cleanups, fishing outings, and monthly meetings open to all.
